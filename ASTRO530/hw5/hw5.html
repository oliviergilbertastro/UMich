1. Gap opening condition

a)

The gap opening timescale is $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{\Delta J}{\left|\frac{\mathrm{d}J}{\mathrm{d}t}\right|}$$

where $\Delta J = 2\pi a w \Sigma \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}l}{\mathrm{d}r}\right|_a w$, with $l=\sqrt{GM_s r}$. With the approximation of an impulse, we can also use

$$\frac{\mathrm{d}J}{\mathrm{d}t}=-\frac{8}{27}\frac{G^2 M_p^2 a \Sigma}{\Omega_p^2 w^3}$$

We can therefore start solving for $t_\mathrm{open}$:

$$\left.\frac{\mathrm{d}l}{\mathrm{d}r}\right|_a = \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}\sqrt{GM_s r}}{\mathrm{d}r}\right|_a = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a}}$$

$$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{\Delta J}{\left|\frac{\mathrm{d}J}{\mathrm{d}t}\right|}$$ $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{2\pi a w \Sigma \left.\frac{\mathrm{d}l}{\mathrm{d}r}\right|_a w}{\frac{8}{27}\frac{G^2 M_p^2 a \Sigma}{\Omega_p^2 w^3}}$$ $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{2\pi a w \Sigma \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a}} w}{\frac{8}{27}\frac{G^2 M_p^2 a \Sigma}{\Omega_p^2 w^3}}$$ $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{27 \pi \Omega_p^2 w^5 \sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a}}}{8G^2 M_p^2}$$

And we know that for a Keplerian orbit at $r=a$, $\Omega_p=\sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a^3}}$: $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{27 \pi \frac{GM_s}{a^3} w^5 \sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a}}}{8G^2 M_p^2}$$ $$t_\mathrm{open} = \frac{27 \pi w^5}{8 M_p^2} \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}}$$

b)

The timescale to close the gap is $t_\mathrm{close} = w^2/\nu$, and using an alpha disk $\nu = \alpha c_s H$ with the assumption the width of the gap $w$ needs to be equal to the scale height of the disk $H$, we can calculate the minimum mass a planet must be to open a gap. We simply need to solve for $M_p$ while equating the opening and closing timescale:

$$t_\mathrm{open} = t_\mathrm{close}$$ $$\frac{27 \pi w^5}{8 M_p^2} \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}} = \frac{w^2}{\nu}$$ $$\frac{27 \pi w^5}{8 M_p^2} \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}} = \frac{w^2}{\alpha c_s H}$$ $$\frac{27 \pi H^4}{8 } \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}} = \frac{M_p^2}{\alpha c_s}$$ $$M_p = \left[\frac{27 \pi H^4 \alpha c_s}{8 } \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}}\right]^{1/2}$$ We can also replace $c_s$ to be $H \Omega_K$, with $\Omega_K=\sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a^3}}$ at $r=a$: $$M_p = \left[\frac{27 \pi H^4 \alpha H\sqrt{\frac{GM_s}{a^3}}}{8 } \sqrt{\frac{M_s^3}{Ga^7}}\right]^{1/2}$$ $$M_p = \left[\frac{27 \pi H^5 \alpha }{8 } \sqrt{\frac{M_s^4}{a^10}}\right]^{1/2}$$ $$M_p = \left[\frac{27 \pi H^5 \alpha M_s^2 }{8a^5 }\right]^{1/2}$$

We have therefore found that, to carve a gap, the planetary mass must be $$M_p \geq \left[\frac{27 \pi H^5 \alpha M_s^2 }{8a^5 }\right]^{1/2}$$

c)

Assuming a characteristic $\frac{H}{r}\sim0.05$, $\alpha\sim 10^{-3}$, and $M_s=M_\odot$, we can calculate the planet mass needed to open a gap:

$$M_p \geq \left[\frac{27 \pi H^5 \alpha M_s^2 }{8a^5 }\right]^{1/2}$$ We can replace the values of $M_s$ and $\alpha$, and use $\frac{H}{r} = \frac{H}{a}\to H=0.05a$:

$$M_p \geq \left[\frac{27 \pi 0.05^5 \cdot 10^{-3}\cdot M_\odot^2 }{8 }\right]^{1/2}$$

We can now calculate this, which gives us $19.17 M_\oplus = 1.12 M_\mathrm{neptune} = 0.06 M_\mathrm{jupiter}$.

We expect the radius of each planet to diminish with time, which would affect the ability of the planet to cool, as the luminosity is proportional to $L\propto R_p^2$. This would therefore reduce the effectiveness of the cooling, which would augment the cooling timescale. The plot in c) would then lose its linear look and show a shallower dependence on the time elapsed.

e)

We would expect the luminosity to get smaller with time, because it is proportional to $L\propto R_p^2 T_\mathrm{int}^4$. We have already explained that the radius will become smaller with time, which already contributes to the diminishing of the luminosity, but the internal temperature will also, by definition, become colder as time goes by, which will contribute to a smaller luminosity. This will make for a much shallower slope than presented on c), with a dependence on time that is not linear, but more asymptotical.

It would be possible to show that exact trend by doing our integration to find $T_\mathrm{int}$ again, but replacing $L$ by its expression dependent on $T_\mathrm{int}$:

$$\int\mathrm{d}T_\mathrm{int} = -\int\frac{4\pi \sigma R_p(t)^2 T_\mathrm{int}^4}{M_p c_v}\mathrm{d}t $$ $$\int \frac{1}{T_\mathrm{int}^4}\mathrm{d}T_\mathrm{int} = -\int\frac{4\pi \sigma R_p(t)^2 }{M_p c_v}\mathrm{d}t $$

This would be a more accurate way to show the evolution of the internal temperature of the planets.

Something interesting I learned from this lecture is that disks around a very inclined planet can be disturbed to the point where they are locally warped, and even orthogonal compared to the rest of the disk. I also knew that we used infrared observations to observe scattered starlight to infer the structure of the disk, but I learned that one of the reason for this is because that scattered light can be observed at a much higher resolution than optical, and even radio light. Alycia Weinberger mentions the resolution of tens of milliarcseconds that is now possible in the infrared is even higher than ALMA's resolution.

Another thing I learned is that the excess of far-IR emission observed that is usually attributed to the dust can actually be caused by the star sometimes. In resolved images, we can see the star has an excess that was found to be caused by stellar flares, not its blackbody continuum.

Additionally, I learned that the collisions between planetesimals grind them together, which creates smaller rocks, which makes the collision probability higher, etc., until the distribution of the planetesimals exhibits a clear majority of the particles to have a micron-scale size. This dust is then disturbed by radiation pressure, which makes their excentricity much higher, and this creates a halo of dust outside the planetesimal belt. This is completely new to me, as I didn't even know there was a halo of dust at a large distance from the star.

c)

From the lectures, we have learned that there is a minimum planetary mass for which a gap will form in the disk that would otherwise stay closed (no gap). From this talk, I learned that it is possible for a system to have a gap, but not around the whole $2\pi$ of the circular orbit. This is interesting, because it shows that there is probably a range of planetary masses that are too small to carve a gap that will last for a complete orbit without the disk covering it completely again, but large enough to still carve a gap that spans the total height of the disk so that there is no gas flowing over/under the planet.
